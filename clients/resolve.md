# Resolve

## App Description
AI-powered habit tracker that gives you personalized daily reflections and coaching to help you stay consistent with your habits. Track daily habits with simple check-ins (habits to build or quit), long-press the Orb for AI reflections and personalized feedback, view stats and streaks, sync health habits with Apple Health. Core promise: build real self-discipline in 21 days.

## Target Audience

### Primary — Men (18–35)
Ambitious young men who know what they want but keep getting in their own way. They're stuck in dopamine loops — procrastination, vaping, endless scrolling — and they're frustrated with themselves because they have goals but no follow-through. They've tried to quit or change before and fallen off. They don't need more motivation, they need structure and accountability.

They watch self-improvement content, gym content, productivity creators, and anything about discipline or mindset. They respond to direct, no-fluff language. They want to be spoken to like someone who gets it — not lectured. The identity they want to step into is someone who is disciplined, consistent, and in control of their life.

**What they're trying to fix:** Vaping, procrastination, wasted time, lack of gym consistency, too much screen time, no morning routine, low self-discipline
**What they want to become:** Someone who shows up every day, builds real habits, and actually follows through on their goals
**Language that works:** "Stop wasting your potential," "you're not lazy, you're stuck," "21 days to change your life," "I rebuilt my discipline," "Day 1 of rebuilding my life"
**Habit examples:** Quit vaping, no junk food, gym every day, no alcohol, wake up at 6am, cold shower, no mindless scrolling, daily reading, deep work session

### Secondary — Women (20–35)
Women who are motivated and want to improve but struggle with consistency. They start habits and fall off, repeat routines for a few days then lose momentum. They care about their health, their skin, their mornings, and their mindset — but life gets in the way. They're not looking for a hardcore discipline app, they want something that feels supportive and keeps them on track.

They watch routine content, glow-up content, wellness creators, and journaling or self-improvement videos. They respond to warm, relatable language and aspirational but achievable framing. The identity they want to step into is someone who is glowing, consistent, and has her life together.

**What they're trying to fix:** Inconsistent routines, skipping skincare, not drinking enough water, poor sleep habits, not moving enough, not journaling consistently
**What they want to become:** Someone with a routine that actually sticks, who feels good every day and shows up for herself
**Language that works:** "How I finally stayed consistent," "the app that changed my routine," "glow-up starts with discipline," "I stopped starting over every Monday"
**Habit examples:** Drink 2L water, skincare morning and night, 20-min walk, no sugar, sleep before midnight, journal, meditate, workout 4x/week, read 10 pages

## App Moments
### Habit Input (Close-Up Shot)
Creator picks up phone, second device films a close-up of their hand ticking off or inputting a habit in the app. ~5–8 seconds. Natural and quick. The app screen shown should relate to the video topic — e.g. a quit vaping habit with streak and progress for a discipline video, or a water/skincare habit for a routine video.

### AI Reflection Orb
Creator picks up phone, long-presses the Orb, AI reflection activates on screen. ~5–7 seconds. Casual, almost like an afterthought. Just show the moment it activates, not the full reflection.

---

## Integration Rules
- NEVER say the app name. Always refer to it as "an app," "the app," or "this app"
- Integration must be subtle — the app is just part of their routine, not a pitch
- Two app moments per video
- Place each app moment right after the tip that sets it up naturally
- Film phone from a second device — not a screen recording. Natural angle, like showing a friend
- Don't slow down for the app — same energy as the rest of the video
- The app is a tool, not the topic

---

## Tone & Style
- Casual, talking to camera, direct eye contact
- Match reference video energy exactly
- Jump cuts between tips, text overlays per tip
- Casual setting (bedroom, living room, desk)
- Vertical 9:16 format always
- The video is about the topic (discipline, glow-up, habits, routine) — the app is just how you stay consistent

---

## Script Adaptation Rules
- Always produce two versions: one for men, one for women. Same structure, different language, habits, and tone
- Keep the script as close to the original as possible — same tone, phrasing, energy
- Trim videos over 2 minutes — cut the weakest tips to keep it tight
- Replace non-trackable tips with trackable daily habits when needed. Only swap if the original tip wouldn't make sense as a daily habit
- Add a journaling/reflection tip if one doesn't already exist — this sets up the AI Orb moment naturally
- If adding a mindset tip, update the video title accordingly
- 4–5 tips is the sweet spot for a ~1:30–2:00 video

### The 21-Day Challenge (Male Version)
The 21-day challenge is central to the male narrative. Resolve gives users a structured 21-day reset where they pick their one main habit to quit or build and do it every day alongside daily AI reflections. When adapting scripts for men, weave in this framing where it fits naturally — the app isn't just a tracker, it's what drives the 21-day transformation. The creator is on a journey, and the app is the engine behind it. "Day 1 of rebuilding my life with Resolve" is the UGC narrative to lean into.

### Identity-Led Language
Scripts should sell an identity, not a feature. Instead of "this app tracks your habits," the framing is "this is how I finally built discipline" or "this is what I use to stay consistent." The viewer should see themselves in the creator — someone who was struggling and found a way through. For men: ambitious but falling short. For women: motivated but not consistent. The app is what bridges that gap.

### Hook Framing
The hook should match the audience and the pain. For men, hooks should be transformative and direct — call out the problem, make them feel seen, then offer the solution. For women, hooks can be aspirational and routine-led — show the outcome first, then explain how you got there. The app never leads the hook. The topic does.
